Introduction

The pre-colonial Philippine society’s early transportation system was its lifeblood. It shaped how communities thrived and survived within an archipelagic environment. Loarca’s Relations of the Philippine Islands noted that mountain communities relied on coastal goods such as fish and salt, just as coastal communities relied on land-grown products. This interdependence was made possible by maritime trade. This necessity produced diverse and sophisticated vessels, from large river canoes to swift vireys and caracoas designed for inter-island navigation. Not only did transportation sustain economic exchange but also influenced culture, as communities preserved their “ancient lore through songs” sung while rowing. Our understanding of this maritime civilization is based primarily on the accounts of Spanish chroniclers such as Antonio de Morga, Francisco Bobadilla, Miguel de Loarca, Francisco de Santa Ines, and Juan de Plasencia and Chinese authors such as Chu-fan-chi. This study focuses on the pre-colonial to early colonial period and is limited to these surviving primary sources, recognizing that they reflect foreign perspectives on indigenous life.

Conclusions

The Archipelagic Identity

Pre-colonial Philippine transportation was the lifeblood of civilization. In an archipelagic environment, waterways dictated both political authority and spiritual life. A datu's power relied on mastering maritime networks, shipbuilding, and trade routes, while animistic rituals ensured safe passages and revered aquatic elements as domains of spirits. Maritime mobility drove the pre-colonial economy and social structure. Inter-island trade exchanged essential goods, such as coastal salt and fish for mountainous rice and cotton, forging networks of mutual dependence and economic specialization. Access to these maritime routes defined social hierarchies, allowing expedition leaders to accumulate wealth, prestige, and influence. Navigating this environment sparked remarkable technological innovation and cultural adaptation. Early Filipinos engineered specialized vessels using advanced lashed-lug techniques, wooden nails, and flexible bejucos. This maritime reality shaped their architecture, seen in houses elevated on stakes, and deeply influenced their cultural identity as they preserved genealogies through songs chanted to the rhythm of rowing.
