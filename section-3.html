Culture and Technology in a Maritime Environment

In the pre-colonial Philippines, transportation shaped both political authority and spiritual life. Leadership depended heavily on mobility and control of the waterways, as communities were connected by seas and rivers, as opposed to being separated Miguel de Loarca observed settlements were organized in barangays, communities that originally referred to boat-based groups. This was a reflection of how governance itself was rooted in maritime movement, and is inherently decentralized. Political authority rested in the hands of the datu, who exercised power within small but independent communities connected by water routes.

“This people always had chiefs, called by them datos, who governed them and were captains in their wars, and whom they obeyed and reverenced.” (Plasencia)

Antonio de Morga further noted that natives “make use of those craft for trading among the islands,” which shows that commerce is driven by mobility, and that alliances and influences are expanded by it. A datu who controlled trade routes and maritime expeditions strengthened his status, due to the close ties of leadership to resource access and networks of exchange with leaders from other communities.

“Those chiefs possessed a great amount of gold, and slaves in proportion to their nobility.” (Bobadilla)
“The chiefs in some villages had also fisheries, with established limits, and sections of the rivers for markets. At these no one could fish, or trade… without paying for the privilege…” (Plasencia)

In addition to authority and power, religious life is just as embedded in this maritime setting. Juan de Plasencia’s Customs of the Tagalogs documented that many natives believed in spirits inhabiting natural elements such as stones, trees, and the waters. Thus, rituals were performed to seek protection and favor from gods and spirits living in these natural elements. Travel, fishing, and warfare were not simply considered physical acts. Pre-colonial Filipinos considered these spiritual undertakings requiring guidance from figures such as the babaylan, who were considered to have more authority than the datus themselves. This was due to the babaylan being shamans who had a connection to the spiritual and ancestral realms. Even cultural memory was preserved through movement, as accounts describe natives keeping “their ancient lore through songs… commonly while playing their oars.”

“They also worshipped the stones, rocks, reefs, and promontories of land which jut into the sea; and made offerings to those of rice, fish, and other like things, or fire their arrows at them in passing.” (Bobadilla)
“Although they had no temples, they had men and women who acted as priests, who were called catalonan by some and babailan by others.” (Plasencia)

While chroniclers described political structures and spiritual practices as separate entities, the cited accounts reveal a deeper connection: authority, belief, and transportation operated within the same aquatically-centered world. Control of waterways shaped governance, while spiritual practices reinforced stability and unity among maritime communities. In this manner, transportation was more than a means of travel: it was a foundation of both power and faith in pre-colonial Philippine society.
