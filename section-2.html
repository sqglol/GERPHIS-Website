In the pre-colonial Philippines, it has been established that maritime transportation dominated daily life due to the natural geography and topography of the Philippines. This means that maritime mobility is essential to get from one location or community to another. It also ensures a larger capacity for moving goods, from water and food to daily necessities. The ships were used to go across rivers, sailing the length and width of it, but also for coastal ventures such as trading with other villages or according to the Chinese sources with other states.

“Trade was conducted both within the islands and with foreign merchants.” (Bobadilla)

The economic side is also important as boats are the main method of transporting goods both inland by using rivers, and coastal settlements. The goods range from wax, cotton, coconuts, and alcoholic beverages and other products locals made. Trade was also seen as prestigious according to Chu-Fan-Chi as celebrations were held for traders upon their return which indicated that wealth was heavily tied to trade and the vast concentration of wealth was to coastal elites. This heavily suggests that mobility allowed these conditions to be commonplace. The trade of human captives is also noted in the Chinese sources as villages and settlements fought, raided and captured people to be sold.

“The customary method of trading was by bartering one thing for another, such as food, cloth, cattle, fowls, lands, houses, fields, slaves, fishing grounds, and palm trees (both nipa and wild). Sometimes a price intervened, which was paid in gold, as agreed upon, or in metal bells brought from China. These bells they regard as precious jewels; they resemble large pans and are very sonorous. They play upon their feasts and carry them to the water in their boats instead of drums and other instruments. There are often delays and terms for certain payments, and bondsmen who intervene and bind themselves, but always with very usurious and excessive profits and interests.” (Morga)

Beyond economic exchange, maritime transportation fundamentally shaped the social structure of pre-colonial Philippine communities. The constant movement between inland and coastal settlements created networks of interdependence, as Loarca noted that mountain inhabitants relied on coastal goods such as fish and salt, while coastal groups depended on inland products. This reciprocity fostered cooperation but also reinforced distinctions between communities based on their role within trade networks. Access to boats and maritime routes influenced social hierarchy, as individuals who controlled vessels or led trading expeditions accumulated wealth, prestige, and influence. Chinese accounts describing celebrations for returning traders further suggest that maritime success was publicly recognized and socially rewarded. In this way, transportation not only enabled economic survival but also shaped patterns of status, leadership, and social mobility within pre-colonial society.
